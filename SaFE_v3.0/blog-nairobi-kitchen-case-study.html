How Nairobi Kitchen Co. Hit 2.4M Views

MM
Mahlet Mulugeta
Social Media Manager
April 28, 2026 10 min read

In March 2025, Nairobi Kitchen Co. came to us with a problem. They were one of Nairobi's best-kept secrets — a farm-to-table restaurant with incredible food, a beautiful space, and a founder with a compelling story. But on social media? Just 3,400 Instagram followers and posts that rarely cracked 100 likes.

Twelve weeks later, their brand documentary had 2.4 million organic views and their follower count had grown by 340%. Here's exactly how we did it.

Week 1-2: Strategy & Research

We started where we always start: with the audience. Through social listening, competitor analysis, and direct interviews with Nairobi Kitchen Co.'s customers, we identified a clear content gap. No one in the East African food space was telling founder stories with cinematic production value. Everyone was posting food photos. No one was making viewers feel something.

Our strategy was built on three pillars:

  • Founder-first storytelling — Put Chef Amara at the centre of everything
  • Cinematic production — Match the quality of the food with the quality of the video
  • Platform-native distribution — Different edits, different platforms, different audiences
"We didn't want to make a restaurant promo. We wanted to make people feel the way Chef Amara feels when she cooks."

Week 3-4: Creative Development

We developed three core concepts. The anchor piece was a 4-minute brand documentary — From Farm to Flame — that told Chef Amara's journey from culinary school in Cape Town to opening her own restaurant in Nairobi. The supporting content included a series of 30-second recipe Reels, behind-the-scenes TikToks, and quote cards for Instagram Stories.

Every piece of content connected back to the same narrative: this isn't just a restaurant, it's a mission to change how Nairobi thinks about food.

Week 5-6: Production

We shot over four days. Two days at the restaurant — capturing the rhythm of service, the care in plating, the warmth of the dining room. One day at the farms where Chef Amara sources her ingredients — golden hour shots of produce being harvested. And one day for formal interviews and the cooking sequences that would anchor the documentary.

Our team of seven — from Niyan's creative direction to Kirubel's camera work to Abenezer's gimbal tracking — worked in seamless coordination. Every shot was composed with multiple platform outputs in mind.

Week 7-9: Post Production

The edit took three weeks. The full documentary went through seven revisions before we locked picture. Sound design was critical — we wanted viewers to hear the sizzle, the chop, the ambient warmth of the kitchen. An original music score was composed by a local Nairobi producer to give the piece an authentic emotional foundation.

From the master edit, we produced 47 deliverables: the documentary, 12 Reels, 8 TikToks, 15 Stories, 6 YouTube Shorts, and 5 quote graphics. Each was optimised for its platform.

Week 10-12: Distribution & Results

We launched with a three-day rollout. Day one: documentary on YouTube and Instagram. Day two: Reels and TikToks. Day three: behind-the-scenes content and community engagement push. The response was immediate.

Within 72 hours, the documentary had passed 500,000 views. By the end of week two: 1.2 million. Final count after 30 days: 2.4 million organic views, 340% follower growth, and a 280% increase in restaurant bookings.

The campaign worked because every element was intentional. The strategy identified the opportunity. The creative captured the emotion. The production delivered the quality. And the distribution made sure the right people saw it at the right time. That's the 360 approach — and that's why it works.
